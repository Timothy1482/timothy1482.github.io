Discovery Project · ECE 1100
Real-Time Audio Spectrum Analyzer

A Python application that captures live microphone input and displays a real-time frequency spectrum visualization — a color-coded bar graph showing frequency amplitudes as sound is captured. The project explores the relationship between analog signals and digital signal processing, connecting to core ECE concepts in signals and systems.

Project Idea & Motivation

As both a musician and an engineer, I have always been fascinated by the science of sound. I chose this project because it sits at the intersection of two things I care about — music and electrical engineering. The idea was to build a tool that makes the invisible visible: sound is all around us, but we rarely get to see its frequency structure in real time. By building a spectrum analyzer from scratch, I could apply ECE concepts like sampling theory and Fourier analysis to something I interact with every day.

Project Progress — Week by Week

Week 1 - Feb 21-28
Researched FFT theory and how continuous audio signals are sampled and quantized. Installed NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib, and SoundDevice. Verified microphone input was accessible via Python with a simple recording test.
Week 2 - Feb 28-Mar 7
Implemented basic audio capture using SoundDevice's InputStream. Learned about sample rate (44,100 Hz), chunk size, and bit depth. Saved and played back a 3-second recording to verify the pipeline was working correctly.
Week 3 - Mar 7-14
Applied FFT processing to live audio chunks using NumPy. Tested with a 440 Hz tone from my phone and confirmed the output showed a clear spike at approximately 440 Hz. Debugged frequency scaling and amplitude normalization.
Week 4 - Mar 14-21
Built real-time visualization using Matplotlib's FuncAnimation. Created a live-updating bar chart refreshing every 40ms. Added color-coding by frequency range: teal for bass (0-300Hz), purple for mids, pink for highs.
Week 5 - Mar 21-28
Polished the visualization — added axis labels, a peak frequency detector, and styled the UI. Tested on voice, music, and clapping to confirm consistent behavior across different sound sources.
Week 6 - Mar 28-Apr 4
Cleaned up and documented the code with comments. Captured screenshots and recorded a demo. Wrote up the project description, challenges faced, and ECE skills gained for the ePortfolio showcase.

Successes & Challenges

Successes
  • Successfully captured and processed live microphone input in real time
  • FFT correctly identified a 440 Hz tone played from my phone
  • Live visualization updates smoothly at ~25 frames per second
  • Peak frequency detector accurately tracks dominant sounds
Challenges
  • Amplitude scaling was off initially — bars were clipping; fixed by tuning the normalization factor
  • Matplotlib animation lagged at first; resolved by adjusting chunk size and animation parameters
  • Library installation on macOS required using pip3 instead of pip

ECE Skills Gained

ADC
Digital Signal Processing
Understanding how analog sound waves are sampled at 44,100 Hz, quantized, and converted to digital data.
FFT
Frequency Domain Analysis
Applying the Fast Fourier Transform to convert time-domain audio into frequency components — a core tool in communications and audio engineering.
RT
Real-Time Processing
Managing streaming data and understanding tradeoffs between buffer size, latency, and frequency resolution in live systems.

Final Thoughts

This project exceeded my expectations. Going in, I understood music intuitively as a musician — but I had never thought about it mathematically. Implementing the FFT and watching it correctly identify a 440 Hz tone in real time was genuinely exciting. It made abstract concepts from signals and systems feel tangible and real. This project has deepened my interest in the Signals & Systems thread and made me curious about future applications in audio engineering and hardware accelerators for DSP workloads. I plan to continue expanding this — potentially adding a spectrogram view and eventually porting the processing to an FPGA.
